How antipsychotics work in schizophrenia: A primer on mechanisms

Cambridge Core 2024 Review

Key Terms

  • schizophrenia - a serious, chronic mental illness that involves hallucinations, delusions, and disorganized thinking and behavior1
  • positive symptoms of schizophrenia consist of "hallucinations, delusions, disorganized speech/behavior" (Meyer 2024)
  • negative symptoms of schizophrenia consist of apathy3 (lack of feeling or interest) and decreased expressiveness
  • cognitive deficits caused by schizophrenia consist of impaired "working memory, processing speed, and executive function" (Meyer 2024)
  • psychosis - a combination of symptoms that distance a person from reality2

Summary

Since each schizophrenia patient presents a unique combination of symptoms, it is difficult to find one one-size-fits-all schizophrenia medication.

Most antipsychotic medication approved before 2024 use dopamine D2 receptor antagonism. The dopamine D2 receptor is a G protein-coupled receptor in the brain that regulate dopamine levels4. In other words, these antipsychotics work by binding onto dopamine D2 receptors, preventing dopamine from binding.

Clozapine is a newer drug that works differently and succeeds at treating the 1/3 of patients that don't respond to dopamine D2 receptor medications. Unfortunately, its exact mechanisms are currently unknown.

Schizophrenia patients who are not treatment resistant respond well to medications that reduce dopamine production in the brain (e.g. dopamine D2 receptor medications). This strategy reduces positive symptoms.
